## Why Couches Sag (And Why It's Fixable)

### The Real Problem

Couch cushions fail in predictable ways:

**Foam compression:**  
Over 5-10 years, foam loses its ability to rebound. The cells collapse and don't recover. This is normal wear.

**Uneven wear:**  
Everyone has their "spot." That cushion compresses while others stay firmer.

**Low-density foam:**  
Many manufacturers use cheap, low-density foam to cut costs. It fails faster.

**Moisture damage:**  
Spills, humidity, and cleaning can break down foam structure.

### Why the Rest of the Couch Is Fine

**Frames:** Quality hardwood frames last 20+ years. Even plywood frames typically outlast cushions.

**Fabric:** Upholstery fabric is designed for abrasion resistance. Unless torn or stained, it lasts longer than foam.

**Zippers and seams:** These rarely fail before foam does.

**The economics:** New couch $800-2,000. New foam $80-200. Same result—comfortable seating.

## Step 1: Remove Old Cushions

### Finding the Zipper

Most cushion covers have zippers:
- **Check the back** of the cushion (most common)
- **Look underneath** (some hide them on the bottom)
- **Check along seams** (invisible zippers blend in)

**No zipper?** You have options:
1. Carefully cut a small opening along a back seam
2. Take to an upholsterer to add zippers ($10-20 per cushion)
3. Consider this a sign to hire a professional for the whole job

### Removing the Old Foam

1. **Unzip completely**—foam compresses, you need full opening
2. **Pull gently**—old foam tears easily
3. **Remove all pieces**—some cushions have multiple foam layers
4. **Save the batting** (optional)—the fluffy layer around foam can be reused
5. **Inspect the cover**—repair any torn seams now

**What you're looking at:**  
Old foam will be yellowed, possibly crumbling, and definitely compressed. Don't be alarmed—this is normal for 5+ year old foam.

## Step 2: Measure Everything

This is the critical step. Measure wrong, and your new foam won't fit.

### Measure the Cover, Not the Old Foam

**Why?** Old foam is compressed and misshapen. Your cover is the size you need to fill.

**What to measure:**
1. **Width:** Left seam to right seam (measure at center)
2. **Depth:** Front seam to back seam (measure at center)
3. **Thickness:** How thick can the foam be? (measure cover height when empty)

**The foam ordering formula:**
- Width = Cover width + ½ inch
- Depth = Cover depth + ½ inch
- Thickness = Cover thickness (or ¼ inch more for seat cushions)

### Understanding Cushion Types

**T-cushions:**  
These have a "T" shape with front extensions. Measure:
- Main rectangle (back portion)
- Width of front extensions
- Where extensions start

**Box cushions:**  
These have vertical sides (gussets). The gusset height is your thickness measurement.

**Knife-edge cushions:**  
These taper to edges. Measure the thickest point.

### Taking Photos

Before you order:
- Photo of cushion cover empty (shows shape)
- Photo of old foam (shows what was inside)
- Photo of any labels or tags

These help if you need to contact customer service.

## Step 3: Choose Your Foam

### Density Decisions

**Medium Density (1.8 lb/ft³)**
- Soft, plush feel
- 3-5 year lifespan
- Best for: Light use, decorative seating
- Price: $

**High Density (2.5 lb/ft³)** — Recommended for Most Couches
- Medium-firm support
- 7-10 year lifespan
- Best for: Daily use, family rooms
- Price: $$

**Luxury Density (2.8+ lb/ft³)**
- Firm, supportive feel
- 10-15 year lifespan
- Best for: Heavy use, plus-size users, longevity
- Price: $$$

**The sweet spot:** Most people want high-density foam. It's comfortable but lasts. Medium density feels great at first but breaks down faster.

### Thickness Guidelines

| Couch Style | Seat Thickness | Back Thickness |
|-------------|----------------|----------------|
| Modern/Minimal | 4-5 inches | 2-3 inches |
| Traditional | 5-6 inches | 3-4 inches |
| Deep/Plush | 6-8 inches | 4-5 inches |
| Tufted | 4-5 inches | 3-4 inches |

**When in doubt:** Match what you removed. If your old foam was 5 inches and comfortable, order 5 inches.

### Adding Dacron Batting

Dacron is a fluffy polyester layer that wraps around foam:

**Benefits:**
- Creates softer "crown" on cushion surface
- Smooths out foam edges
- Protects foam from friction
- Easier to stuff into covers

**Thickness:** ½ to 1 inch layer

**Cost:** $10-20 for a sofa set

**Recommendation:** Worth it for comfort, especially on seat cushions.

## Step 5: Installation Day

### Unboxing

**When your foam arrives:**
1. Open packages immediately (foam needs to breathe)
2. Let foam expand for 2-4 hours if compressed
3. Match pieces to cushions before starting
4. Lay out Dacron batting if using

### Wrapping with Dacron (Optional but Recommended)

1. **Cut batting** slightly larger than foam piece
2. **Wrap foam** like a present
3. **Secure with spray adhesive** or simply tuck edges
4. **Don't wrap too tight**—you want loft, not compression

### Stuffing the Cushions

**The technique:**
1. **Open zipper fully**
2. **Work foam into corners first**—this is the hardest part
3. **Push gradually**—don't force it
4. **Adjust as you go**—foam shifts, keep repositioning
5. **Leave room to zip**—push foam away from zipper line before closing

**Pro tips:**
- Work on a large flat surface
- Have a second person help with large cushions
- If foam is tight, try compressing it slightly while zipping
- Don't worry about perfection—it settles with use

### The First Sit Test

New foam feels different:
- **Firm at first**—it softens with use (break-in period: 1-2 weeks)
- **Higher seat**—you'll sit taller than on worn-out foam
- **Even support**—no more sliding to the middle

**This is normal.** Your couch will feel "new" again.

## Step 6: Break-In and Care

### The First Two Weeks

**What to expect:**
- Foam softens slightly as cells break in
- Cushions settle into optimal shape
- You adjust to new seat height

**What to do:**
- Rotate cushions weekly (if not attached)
- Fluff and reshape after sitting
- Vacuum covers to remove loose fibers

### Long-Term Care

**Monthly:**
- Rotate cushions (swap left/right, front/back)
- Vacuum surfaces
- Fluff to redistribute foam

**Annually:**
- Deep clean covers
- Inspect foam for wear
- Consider adding more Dacron if cushions feel flat

**Daily habits that extend life:**
- Don't jump on cushions
- Don't stand on cushions (knees concentrate force)
- Rotate your sitting spot (don't always sit in the same place)

## Troubleshooting

### "My cushions are too firm"

**Wait 2 weeks.** New foam is always firmer than broken-in foam.

**If still too firm:**
- Your foam may be too dense—next time order medium density
- Add more Dacron batting on top
- Cushions will soften over months, not days

### "My cushions are too soft/saggy"

**You may have:**
- Ordered too low density
- Didn't add enough thickness
- Have a frame problem (springs broken, not foam issue)

**Solutions:**
- Replace with higher density foam
- Add a foam topper layer
- Check couch frame for structural issues

### "Cushions won't zip closed"

**Common causes:**
- Foam is slightly too large (trim carefully with sharp knife)
- Foam isn't positioned correctly (reposition, work into corners)
- Old zipper is failing (replace zipper or use safety pins as temporary fix)

### "Corners won't fill out"

**Solutions:**
- Work foam into corners more aggressively
- Add small foam pieces in corners before main foam
- Your foam may be too small—contact supplier about sizing

## FAQ

**Q: How long does this project take?**  
A: Measuring and ordering: 30 minutes. Installation: 2-3 hours for a full sofa. Total time: One afternoon.

**Q: Can I do this if my cushion covers don't have zippers?**  
A: Yes, but it's harder. You can carefully cut a seam and sew it back, or take covers to an upholsterer to add zippers ($10-20 each).

**Q: What's the total cost for a standard 3-cushion sofa?**  
A: $150-250 for foam and batting. Compare to $800-1,500 for a new sofa of similar quality.

**Q: How long will new foam last?**  
A: High-density foam lasts 7-10 years with normal use. Luxury density lasts 10-15 years.

**Q: Can I wash my cushion covers?**  
A: Check the tag. Many can be machine washed gentle cycle. Air dry—heat damages foam and can shrink covers.

**Q: What if I measure wrong?**  
A: Contact your foam supplier immediately. Most can trim foam slightly if it's too large. Too small usually requires reordering.

**Q: Is this cheaper than reupholstering?**  
A: Yes. Reupholstering includes new fabric and labor ($800-2,000). Foam replacement keeps your existing covers.

**Q: Can I add more foam to make cushions thicker?**  
A: Only if your covers have room. Measure cover capacity first. Overstuffing causes stress on seams and zippers.
